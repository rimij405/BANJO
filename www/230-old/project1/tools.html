The Guitar Toolset

Empowering you with the tools to succeed

Any aspiring guitarist will add the following skills to their collection before they master the guitar.

Let's look at some of those skills!

1. Learn how to hold a pick
(and play safely).

A pick or a plectrum is used to play the guitar. Playing with one's fingers can lead to calloused or cut fingertips! By using a pick whenever possible, players can prevent injury to themselves. Safety comes first!

2. Learn how to use a metronome
(and stay on-beat).

A metronome is imperative for learning how to play in-time with any song. By being in time and staying with the rhythm of any song you wish to play, you'll find it easier to improvise your own melodies as your skills progress.

3. Learn how to read tablature
(and play along).

Guitar tablature and chord sheets are a way of sharing song compositions for other guitarists to play without using classical sheet music notation. While some tabs provide fingerboard positions, other provide the placement of chords alongside any lyrics that the song might have. Most pop songs today have had tablatures created by other guitarists and shared online!

An example of a good guitar tablature can be found for the song, "Do I Wanna Know," by the Arctic Monkeys at GuitareTab.com.

4. Learn sheet music notation
(and make any kind of music).

Sheet music notation is a special kind of language used to transcribe and share musical pieces. When musicians traditionally composed music, they did so using sheet music notation. Before guitar tablature and chord sheets were prevalent, western compositions were only commonly accessbile through sheet music notation.

Today, sheet music allows artists to share pieces with other musicians - even if they use different instruments. If your favorite song was composed for the piano, using sheet music, you'd be able to play the same melody on your guitar, given your skill permits.

There exists many online software solutions that allow guitarists to create and share their own musical compositions!

6. Practice the chord progression
(and play any kind of music).

A chord is a grouping of notes - typically three or more - that, when played together, make the basis of a harmony.

Interestingly enough, while chords are prevalent in West African, Oceanian, and Western music, chords are largely absent from the music in many other parts of the world! Feel free to experiment - chords are supposed to make it easier for you to compose music but you are not at all limited to Western interpretations of music.

7. Practice with flash cards!
(or with similar study techniques).

What do flash cards, Quizlet, and ANKI have in common? They're all ways for you to study the guitar, sheet music, chord formation, and musical terms. Any skill is lost without practice and review! Using these sites, you can keep your skills at their peak.
