The Guitar

What is a guitar?

A guitar is a stringed musical instrument with a fretted fingerboard and either six or twelve strings. Guitars are played by plucking or strumming fingers or a plectrum (pick).

What kinds of guitars are there?

There are three types of guitars: acoustic, electric, and acoustic-electric hybrids.

Acoustic guitars have a hollow body, allowing the sound to naturally resonate and amplify.

Acoustic guitars transmit sound without the use of electronic pickups.

Electric guitars are usually solid-body guitars with very little acoustic resistance.

Electric guitars transmit sound with magnetic pickups beneath the strings.

Acoustic-electric guitars are semi-hollow with electrical pickups;
they're useful for bridging the gap between genres.

Acoustic-electric hybrids can work as acoustic guitars or as electric guitars.

Guitar Anatomy

Look at the "bits."

Modern guitars can vary in size and purpose but they all have the same general pieces.

Let's look at some of those pieces!

Headstock - The head or headstock is at the end of the neck where the strings stop. The tuners are here as well.

Tuners - Tuners, located on the headstock, are used to change the pitch of a string by loosening or tightening them. Too tight and the strings will snap!

Nut - The nut is a piece found at the end of the neck that reinforces its attachment to the headstock and keeps the strings in place.

Neck - The bar sticking out of the body, along which the string runs. It connects the headstock to the body.

Frets - The spaces between the strips of metal on the fretboard. When asked to place your fingers on a particular fret, however, it is referring to the fretwire on the bottom of a particular fret.

Fingerboard/Fretboard - The fretboard, on the flatterside of the neck, is where you place your fingers to play.

Fretwires - The small strips of metal that are spread out along the fretboard.

Position Markers - The dots on the fretboard are visual indicators for the 3rd, 5th, 7th, and 9th frets. The 12th fret is usually marked in a different way, indicating the start of the pattern's repetition.

Body - The body is the main part of the guitar. Here you'll find the bridge, any volume/tone controls, and other important components to the instrument.

Scratch Plate - The scratch plate protects the body from scratching and holds any electronics in place.

Pick-up(s) - Electric guitars have grouping of magnets (called "pickups") that lie beneath the strings. Pickups use their magnets to measure the changes in the magnetic field made by the moving strings. (Pickups come in two styles: single coil and humbucker).

Volume Knob(s) - On an electric guitar, this controls the volume of the electric signal.

Tone Knob(s) - The tone knobs control the amount of bass or treble the electric guitar makes.

Bridge - The anchor fastening the strings to the body. When changing strings, this is where the, "ball," sits.

Rose - The decoration around a sound hole is called a rose!

Sound Hole - Acoustic and acoustic-electric guitars use this hole to allow the sound to exit the guitar after it's bounced around inside the body. Acoustic guitars tend to be larger than electric guitars to allow this buildup and transfer of sound.

Strap Pin - Some guitars will have a strap pin - this is where the guitar straps fit on!

Pick-up Selector - Most electric guitars have more than one pickup. This selector allows the player to choose between one or more than one selector at a time.

Input Jack - It's not pictured here, but, this is where an electric guitar is plugged into an amplifier. Loose cables can be an issue as they generate a lot of noise!
